# Woven Taxonomy

A design philosophy of systematic naturalism — where organic structure meets analytical precision.

---

## Philosophy

Woven Taxonomy begins with a paradox: the most natural things in the world submit to the most rigorous geometry. A cotton fiber under magnification reveals lattice structures as precise as any architect's grid. Silk filaments arrange themselves in crystalline formations. Wool scales interlock with mechanical perfection. This philosophy takes that hidden order and makes it visible — treating natural materiality as a subject worthy of the same meticulous cartographic attention we give to celestial bodies or molecular bonds. Every mark placed with the painstaking care of a scientific illustrator cataloging a new species.

The palette is earth before it becomes soil: raw umber, sun-bleached linen, the dusty rose of terracotta dust caught in late-afternoon light, the cool slate of river stone. Color is never decorative. It functions as material evidence — each tone a specimen collected from the natural world, applied with the precision of someone who has spent countless hours calibrating exactly the right warmth, the right weight, the right breath between hues. Gradients do not exist; only the honest edges where one material meets another.

Form follows the logic of the loom. Vertical and horizontal axes dominate, but with the subtle irregularity of handcraft — threads that are almost parallel, intervals that are almost equal. The grid is present but alive, breathing with the imperfection of organic growth. Circles appear only as cross-sections of fiber. Rectangles stack and weave like warp and weft. Negative space is not emptiness but the air trapped between threads — structurally essential, deliberately measured. Every spatial relationship the product of deep expertise and labored refinement, as if each element were positioned, reconsidered, and repositioned a hundred times.

Typography enters as specimen labeling — clinical, thin, lowercase, placed with the reverence of a museum placard beside a textile fragment. Words are rare and functional: a material name, a percentage, a classification marker. They float in space like annotations in a field guide, never dominating, always deferring to the visual composition. When text does appear at scale, it becomes fiber itself — letters constructed from the same visual vocabulary of thread and weave, rendered at the master level where craft becomes indistinguishable from art.

Repetition is scripture. A single motif — a thread, a cross-hatch, a small square — repeated with variations so subtle they reward sustained viewing. The accumulation of these marks creates density and meaning, like the way individual fibers become fabric only through patient, systematic layering. The final work must appear as though it took countless hours of painstaking attention: an artifact from an imaginary discipline where someone has devoted their life to proving that the everyday materials touching our skin are worthy of the same reverence we reserve for constellations.
